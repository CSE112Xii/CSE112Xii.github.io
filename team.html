Simon Hestermann

Team Lead

As a team lead, you will be asking yourself what your concrete contribution to the project was, since you are going to spend most of your time communicating! You will have to make decisions, approve concepts, and most importanly, make sure your team is headed in the right direction. While you shouldn't hide behind being the organizer, neither will you have the chance to hind behind code like some of your teammates will. Always have an overview of what is going on, and, which turns out to be the hardest part, find a balance between criticizing people for not meeting their promises and keeping them motivated at the same time. If you prefer being shy or talking to your computer, this role is not for you. However, if you feel comfortable with taking the responsibility for everyone, go for it!

Jose Rodriguez

Team Lead

Be aware of your team’s and your own strengths and weaknesses. You are not boss, but there will be times that you must make tough decisions. For example, switching from React to Vue was a difficult decision since we had an experienced React developer which would’ve sped our frontend development. Listen and take a lot of notes when meeting with the customer. Ask a lot of questions, but do your homework beforehand when meeting with the customer. Keep an open mind and don’t be afraid to ask your teammates, professor, and tutors for guidance. Take ownership of the work you do and most importantly have fun the process.

Victor He

Backend Developer

Do you believe in labels? If you do you are in for a surprise. Most job labels do a poor job in portraying what is needed of you. Quite often are expected to be competent in skills not directly relevant to your role. I used to think software engineering and programming were synonymous. In reality, programming is but a step in the software engineering process. And more often than not, it is in our job description to make the decision to not code in favor of using someone else’s code. Here is a glimpse of what you can expect Backend Developer: Researching, constructing diagrams, planning, reporting, discussing, arguing, agreeing, documenting, presenting, coding, and then testing. To thrive in a team environment, you need to hone your soft skills in additional to your technical skills. One final note, you should really consider the wisdom professor Powell has to offer. Ignore your default inclinations momentarily, and really evaluate and apply his tips.

Jeremy Park

Documentation Team

Documentation presents information, goals, aspiration, and meaning to what your project will become. Your team's success is based upon how well your client, users, and your team understand the project. As a documentation team member, it is best to start on creating a journal and progress throughout the starting development of the project. As this will present your beginning development, instructions, changes, and thoughts. Communication and patience is key. Keep up-to-date with your team throughout each stage of development. Your team will be roadblocked with difficult development of the project, so it is important to stay patient and understand their work, and encourage them. Furthermore, the continuing success of the project can be built upon your future/new developers joining your team. Knowing why changes were made and failure occurred is important for future developers to understand. The clarity, guide-through, and extendability of your documentation can provide great understanding and success to everyone who revolves around the project.

Justin Kim
